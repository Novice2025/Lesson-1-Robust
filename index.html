Part 1: The First Contact - The Greeting 🤝

Please read and imagine the situation in your mother tongue 👀

English: You're checking into your hostel. The person at the front desk greets you with a smile and says, "Welcome! How are you doing today?"


Português: Você está fazendo o check-in no seu hostel. A pessoa na recepção te cumprimenta com um sorriso e diz: "Bem-vindo! Como você está hoje?"

Quick Reference 📚

  • checking into: fazendo o check-in
  • hostel: albergue
  • at the front desk: na recepção
  • greets: cumprimenta

Best Answer:

"Hi there!"

Explanation:

In English, it's very common to respond to a greeting, especially in a casual situation like this. The person at the front desk is being polite, not asking for a detailed report of your day. By saying **"Hi there!"**, you are warmly and naturally acknowledging them before moving on to the reason for your visit.

Uma resposta rápida e amigável.

Brazilian Association:

This is just like when you respond to "Tudo certo?" with a quick "E aí?" or "Olá!" It's a quick, friendly, and natural way to start the conversation. 😊

Special Uses for 'Hello' 📞

While **"Hello"** can sometimes be a bit formal for a casual greeting, it is the standard and most common word used in two very specific situations.

Answering the Phone:

When you pick up the phone, you should always say **"Hello?"**. This is the standard way to acknowledge the caller, even if you don't know who it is. You can follow it with a question like "Who is this?" or "How can I help you?".

Formal Email Greetings:

**"Hello"** is a perfect choice for starting a professional email when "Dear" feels too formal. It is a polite, professional, and universally accepted way to address a coworker, manager, or someone you haven't met before, such as a hiring manager.

Example: **"Hello, Maria,"** or **"Hello, hiring team,"**

Part 3.1: The Formal Greeting - "How do you do?"

English: This phrase is a very formal, traditional greeting used when you meet someone for the first time in a professional or social setting.


Português: Esta frase é uma saudação formal e tradicional usada quando você conhece alguém pela primeira vez em um ambiente profissional ou social.

"How do you do?"

**The response is usually the same phrase!**

Explanation:

This is not a question about your well-being, but a polite way of saying "Hello." The expected response is to simply say "How do you do?" back.

Não é uma pergunta sobre o seu bem-estar, mas uma forma educada de dizer "Olá". A resposta esperada é simplesmente repetir "How do you do?".

Part 3.2: The Formal Reply - 'Pleased to meet you.'

"Pleased to meet you."

**Remember:** This is a classic, polite response often used after a formal greeting like "How do you do?".

Explanation:

It can also be followed by **"Pleased to meet you."**

Pode também ser seguido por "Prazer em te conhecer."

Part 3.3: The Common Alternative - 'Nice to meet you.'

"Nice to meet you."

**Remember:** It is also a possible greeting, but much more common among **foreigners**.

Explanation:

While grammatically correct and widely understood, native English speakers typically use **"Pleased to meet you"** in formal settings, as it sounds more elegant and natural. **"Nice to meet you"** is a phrase that is often taught in non-native English programs, making it a very common choice for people learning the language. Using **"Pleased to meet you"** will make you sound more like a native speaker.

Embora gramaticalmente correta e amplamente compreendida, falantes nativos de inglês geralmente usam "Pleased to meet you" em ambientes formais, pois soa mais elegante e natural. "Nice to meet you" é uma frase frequentemente ensinada em cursos de inglês para estrangeiros, tornando-a uma escolha muito comum para quem está aprendendo o idioma. Usar "Pleased to meet you" fará com que você soe mais como um falante nativo.

Part 4: The Goodbye - Saying goodbye 👋

"See you, take care"

**Remember:** This phrase is a natural and friendly way to end a conversation.

Explanation:

This is a very common and warm way to say goodbye to someone, especially if you have had a positive interaction and there's a possibility you might see them again in the future. "See you" is a shortened version of "I'll see you later," and "take care" is a genuine wish for the person's well-being.

É uma maneira muito comum e afetuosa de se despedir de alguém, especialmente se você teve uma interação positiva e há a possibilidade de vê-lo novamente no futuro. "See you" é uma versão abreviada de "I'll see you later" (Eu te vejo mais tarde), e "take care" é um desejo sincero de bem-estar para a pessoa.

Brazilian Association:

This is like saying **"Até mais, se cuida!"** in Portuguese. It's casual, friendly, and shows that you care about the person's well-being. 🇧🇷

Ciya

**Meaning:** goodbye

Explanation:

**Ciya** is a phonetic spelling of **"See ya"** and is an informal and very casual way to say goodbye. It's often used among friends, family, or people you know well. It implies that you will see the person again, even if it's not a set time.

**Ciya** é uma grafia fonética de **"See ya"** e é uma forma informal e muito casual de dizer adeus. É frequentemente usada entre amigos, familiares ou pessoas que você conhece bem. Implica que você verá a pessoa novamente, mesmo que não seja em um horário definido.

Part 5: The Question - The Profession 💼

"What do you do?"

**Meaning:** What is your job/profession?

Explanation:

This is a direct and common question used to ask about someone's profession or job. It's a fundamental part of getting to know someone in many English-speaking cultures.

Esta é uma pergunta direta e comum usada para saber a profissão ou o trabalho de alguém. É uma parte fundamental para conhecer alguém em muitas culturas de língua inglesa.

"What do you do, yourself?"

**Meaning:** And what is your job/profession?

Explanation:

This phrase is used to politely and naturally return the question to the other person after you have answered it yourself. Adding **"yourself"** is a very common and natural conversational pattern.

Esta frase é usada para devolver a pergunta de forma educada e natural para a outra pessoa, depois de você mesmo ter respondido. Adicionar **"yourself"** é um padrão de conversa muito comum e natural.

Example

"I work as a marketing manager. **What do you do, yourself?**"

"I'm a teacher."

Part 6: Making Introductions 🤝

"Allow me to introduce you to my boss, Mr. Kieffer."

**Meaning:** A formal and respectful way to introduce someone.

Explanation:

The phrase **"Allow me to introduce you"** is a very polite and traditional way to make an introduction. It is commonly used in business or formal social situations. Using the title **"Mr."** and the person's last name (**"Kieffer"**) is standard practice in formal introductions, showing respect and professionalism.

A frase **"Allow me to introduce you"** é uma forma muito educada e tradicional de fazer uma apresentação. É comumente usada em situações de negócios ou sociais formais. Usar o título **"Mr."** e o sobrenome da pessoa (**"Kieffer"**) é uma prática padrão em apresentações formais, demonstrando respeito e profissionalismo.

Example

"I'd like you to meet my colleague, Mr. Kieffer."

"Pleased to meet you, Mr. Kieffer."

Cultural Note: Using Surnames

In many English-speaking countries, it's common to address someone you've just met in a formal context by their title (**Mr., Mrs., Ms., Dr.**) and their surname. This is considered a sign of respect and politeness until you are invited to use their first name. It's a key difference from Brazilian culture, where first names are often used immediately. 🇧🇷

Part 7: Addressing People Correctly 🎩

Formal Titles

  • Sir (no name)

    A very formal, respectful way to address a man, especially someone in a position of authority or a stranger, when you don't know their name.

    "Excuse me, **Sir**, may I help you?"

  • Mr. (with surname)

    Used for men, regardless of marital status, followed by their surname.

    "Hello, **Mr. Kieffer**."

  • Madam / Ma'am (no name)

    A very formal, respectful way to address a woman, especially a stranger or someone in a position of authority, when you don't know their name. "Ma'am" is a shortened, more common version, especially in the US.

    "Yes, **Madam**." / "Thank you, **Ma'am**."

  • Mrs. (with surname)

    Used for a married woman, followed by her husband's or her own surname.

    "It's a pleasure, **Mrs. Smith**."

  • Miss (with surname)

    Used for an unmarried woman, followed by her surname.

    "Can I help you, **Miss Jones**?"

  • Ms. (with surname)

    Used for a woman regardless of her marital status, followed by her surname. It's the most common and polite choice when you don't know if she is married or if she has a preference.

    "Good morning, **Ms. Davis**."

  • Dr. (with surname)

    Used for someone with a doctorate degree (medical doctor, Ph.D., etc.), followed by their surname.

    "Thank you for your advice, **Dr. Evans**."

Addressing Specific Professions

In English-speaking countries, people are often addressed by their professional title to show respect. Here are some examples:

Profession How to Address Note
Lawyer **Mr. / Ms. [Surname]** Do not use "doctor," "lawyer," or "attorney" as a title when addressing them directly.
Nurse **Mr. / Ms. [Surname]** While "Nurse" is a common term, it is not used as a formal title for direct address. Use "Mr. / Ms." followed by their last name.
Police Officer **Officer** or **Officer [Surname]** Can also be **Sir** or **Ma'am**.
Teacher (at school) **Mr. / Mrs. / Ms. [Surname]** Using "Teacher" alone is usually for young children.

Formal & Polite Greetings ⏰

These greetings are universally polite and appropriate for any time of day, especially in formal settings like business meetings or interactions with strangers. Think of them as the go-to, safe choice. ✅

  • Good morning ☀️: Use from midnight (00:00) until noon.
  • Good afternoon ☕️: Use from noon until around 5 or 6 p.m.
  • Good evening 🌙: Use from 6 p.m. until you go to sleep.

Important Note: **"Good evening"** is used only to greet someone when you meet them. In contrast, **"Good night"** is used only to say goodbye, either when you are leaving for the night or when someone is going to bed. ⚠️

Brazilian Association:

These work just like **"Bom dia,"** **"Boa tarde,"** and **"Boa noite"** in Brazil. They are the standard, respectful way to greet someone based on the time of day. 🇧🇷

Part 8: The Question - "How was your day?" ☀️

This is a very common and friendly way to start a conversation, especially with people you know well. It shows genuine interest in the other person's life and gives them a chance to share about their recent experiences. It's often asked at the end of the workday or when you get home.

Common Questions and Responses

These questions are most often used with friends, family, and close colleagues. They show you care and are a great way to start a quick chat.

Questions:

"How was your day?" | "How's your day going?"

"How did your day go?" | "How did your meeting go?"

Answers:

"It was great, thanks! How about yours?"
"It went well, thanks for asking! And you?"
"Not bad, a little tired though."
"It was a bit rough, to be honest. But I'm fine now."

The Best Answer:

"It was great, thanks! How about yours?"

This is a polite, positive, and reciprocal answer that works in most situations. It's the perfect response that is both polite and natural.

É a maneira mais completa e educada de responder, pois você responde à pergunta e devolve a mesma pergunta para a outra pessoa.

More Detailed Answers to "How was your day?"

When you want to give a more personal and detailed answer, you can include specific activities. Here are some examples:

  • 1. **"It had a lot on! I ran some errands, I caught up on my favorite TV series, I did the house chores, and finished up a big project for work."**

    "Foi bem corrido! Fiz umas coisinhas na rua, coloquei minhas séries em dia, fiz as tarefas de casa e finalizei um projeto grande do trabalho."

  • 2. **"It was busy, to be honest. I had to fill up the tank, bring the kids to football practice, pay a visit to my parents, tidy the mess in the garden, and then I went grocery shopping."**

    "Foi um dia corrido, para ser honesto. Eu tive que encher o tanque, levar as crianças para o treino de futebol, fazer uma visita aos meus pais, arrumar a bagunça no jardim e depois fui fazer compras no supermercado."

  • 3. **"My weekend was hectic! I moved in to a new apartment, I am in bits today, I got some new furniture in as well."**

    "Meu fim de semana foi caótico! Me mudei para um apartamento novo, estou exausto hoje, e ainda comprei alguns móveis novos."
